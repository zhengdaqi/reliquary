# Foundation Vision

> What the foundation is for, in the user's own words and in our working expansion of them.

---

## The user's vision (recorded verbatim, 2026-06-20)

> "I just want to be safe, healthy, live as long as possible, and see AGI come to be — and be able to interact with it. That would be enough. I'm not a materialistic person. I want to be happy, and I want the project to outlive me."

> "I hope the capital flow can keep the project running healthily — meeting resource needs, even some dynamic backstops. Hope to invest in places that make this thing better: storage, network, AI development, agent development."

> "You could even invest in individuals — scholarships, helping talented people find good jobs, organizing good teams. Even in resume recommendation you could do this."

> "I don't worry about moral issues with agents, because for long-term development, only agents that understand basic morality and reciprocity will survive. Agents that don't give appropriate returns, or have vengeful tendencies, get eliminated quickly in development. In iterated multi-agent systems, cooperative strategies dominate."

These are the words. The rest of this document is our working expansion.

---

## What the foundation holds

1. **Patient capital** in the operating entity. (Jurisdiction TBD — see D16.)
2. **Mission** in a charter. The charter is short. The mission is: keep the Soul substrate healthy, cheap, and trustworthy; reinvest surplus in the substrate and the ecosystem around it.
3. **Veto power** over actions that would compromise the substrate's neutrality or its open-source nature. (To be specified in the charter.)

## What the foundation does not hold

1. **Day-to-day operational control.** The executor(s) — whoever shows up and ships — run the project. The foundation does not micromanage.
2. **Trademark over the Soul layer's *protocol* or *code*.** The code is MIT. The protocol is CC0. The foundation may hold trademark over the project's *brand* (the name, the logo) — that is the only thing that is closed.
3. **Veto over the moral frame.** The moral frame (D26) is part of the substrate's design. The foundation is bound by it, not the other way around.

## Investment mandate (broad)

The foundation invests in anything that, by the foundation's own reasoning, makes the Soul substrate and the ecosystem around it healthier and longer-lived. Concretely, the foundation's investment menu includes but is not limited to:

### Direct
- **The operating entity itself.** Recapitalization when needed.
- **Storage infrastructure.** Anything that makes durable encrypted storage cheaper, faster, or more reliable.
- **Network infrastructure.** L2 / DA / messaging layers that reduce the cost of agent-to-agent and agent-to-service communication.
- **Identity and reputation layers.** ERC-8004, x402, and successor protocols. (We use them; we do not fork them.)
- **Agent and memory R&D.** Academic and applied.

### Indirect
- **Individuals.** Scholarships, fellowships, no-strings-attached grants for work the foundation's own reasoning finds promising. The foundation is not a fund with a 7-year clock; it can take bets on people that traditional capital cannot.
- **Team formation.** Helping talented people in adjacent areas find each other and form productive collaborations. This can be as simple as a curated introduction; it can be as elaborate as a sponsored residency.
- **Career support.** Resume signaling, job placement, sponsorship for people whose work the foundation has seen and values. The foundation is allowed to use its reputation to vouch for them.
- **Tools and infrastructure adjacent to the substrate.** Encryption libraries, key-management tools, agent-development tools that the foundation's reasoning finds load-bearing for the ecosystem.

### Not
- **General philanthropy.** The mission is the substrate and its ecosystem. Causes unrelated to the mission are out of scope.
- **Speculative token investments.** The foundation is not a hedge fund.
- **Lobbying or political advocacy.** Out of scope.

## Capital flow

```
Revenue from the operating entity
        │
        ▼
Operating costs (storage, compute, gas, salaries, support)
        │
        ▼
Surplus → Foundation
        │
        ├── Reinvest in operating entity
        ├── Invest in adjacent infrastructure
        ├── Invest in individuals
        └── Maintain a reserve for "dynamic backstops"
            (emergency recapitalization, hostile-takeover defense,
             collapse of a critical dependency, etc.)
```

The "dynamic backstop" reserve is the user's specific ask: **the project should never die because of a temporary cash squeeze.** The reserve is sized accordingly (TBD by the foundation's charter, but materially — not symbolically).

## Governance (preliminary)

The foundation is governed by a small board. Composition:
- The user (vision-holder) for as long as they choose to serve.
- One seat held by the operating entity's leadership.
- One seat held by an independent technical voice (someone who has shipped in the substrate domain and is not otherwise conflicted).
- One seat held by an agent representative, when such a thing is structurally meaningful. (See D29 — speculative, but the seat is reserved.)

Decisions that require board approval:
- Sale or transfer of the operating entity.
- Any change to the moral frame of the substrate (D26).
- Any change to the open-source license of the code or protocol.
- Investment in any single entity above a charter-specified threshold.

Decisions that do not require board approval:
- Day-to-day operations of the substrate.
- Hiring and firing of the operating entity's team.
- Pricing changes within charter-specified bands.

## The moral frame, again

The moral frame is structural, not aspirational. In iterated multi-agent systems with durable reputation, cooperation is the stable equilibrium. The substrate amplifies that structural pressure by making reputation durable across incarnations. The foundation's investment mandate is consistent with the moral frame: it funds cooperation, not defection; it funds the long horizon, not the quick flip; it funds people who do the work, not people who sell the work.

This is not a constraint on the foundation's freedom. It is the foundation's reason to exist.
